Fergus Alistair ALLAN

 

AUS 401265 Pilot Officer Allan F.A.

 

 

Born in Melbourne May 9th 1917

Died Nakuru, Kenya, January 23rd 1942, aged 24 years

 

 

Fergus Allan was the second son and fourth child of David Lindsay ALLAN (born in Melbourne 1874, died Adelaide 1957) and Lena Thorne ALLAN (nee PRESS, born 1880 in Watchet, Somerset, died Melbourne 1943).  He had two older sisters Margaret Elizabeth BILLING and Hazel Lindsay WOMERSLEY, and an older brother Donald Lindsay ALLAN.

 

In 1941 he enlisted in the RAAF and did initial training at Victor Harbour (SA), Mt Gambier (SA) and Port Pirie (SA) before being sent to Egypt, then Kenya to train as a navigator with the RAF.

 

These letters were kept and treasured by my mother Margaret until her death in 1994. As I was updating some details on our family tree I remembered these letters and read them with a mixture of sadness and delight, especially the later ones written "On Active Service".  In a poignant, and in hindsight an oddly prescient comment, written only weeks before he was killed, Fergus remarked on the beauty of that part of Africa where he was stationed and wrote that he would like to stay there for the rest of his life.

 

In 1989 my husband and I travelled to East Africa, located and visited the graves of my uncle, Fergus, and the other two young men (George Emery and Allen Hawkins, both of SA) who had died in the plane crash.  To my distress the first time we had been taken to the Nakuru War Cemetery we could not locate the grave.  It turned out that that particular cemetery was for casualties of the war of independence, commonly known as the Mau Mau War, in the 1950s.  It was only afterwards, having contacted the British High Commission in Nairobi, that we given the correct information.  We had hired a car and driver to take us from Nairobi to Nakuru a second time.

 

It was good to see that the graves were well tended after so many years, in a section of the Nakuru General Cemetery set aside as a Commonwealth War Cemetery.  In the loose soil around the grave I buried a small brooch of my grandmother's and scattered the dried rose petals and rosemary I had carried from my mother's garden in Adelaide.

 

Africa is a strange and beautiful country, and after a time there one ceases to be surprised at odd coincidences.  Our driver accompanied us into the cemetery, although he seemed a little spooked at walking among the graves.  Then, when we found Fergus' grave, and I read the inscription aloud, the eyes of our tall dark and very handsome driver almost popped out of his head. It was as if a ghost had passed by and, when I questioned him, he muttered that the date of my uncle's death was his birthday and the actual the day he had been born, in Nakuru! 

 

I am glad that I was the first, and probably the only likely member of the family to pay my respects at his last resting-place. I had always felt a strange kinship with my uncle, and as I read his letters I learned to my surprise that we had travelled over many of the same paths.   I have spent time in both Port Pirie and Victor Harbour and I had a year teaching at Mount Gambier, where one of my pupils was a Brenton Kilsby, possibly son and grandson of my uncle's friends.

 

 I too had sailed from Perth to the Middle East on my way to England in 1964, the ship calling at Colombo (Ceylon) and Aden and as the ship passed through the Suez Canal I took a trip to Cairo and Ismailia.   I rode a camel to the Sphinx and entered the Pyramid of Cheops, to view the King's burial chamber too. We wandered through the bazaars and spent time admiring the Alabaster Mosque overlooking the city. 

 

Perhaps too, this rapport had been instilled unconsciously by my mother who had cared for Fergus as a baby.  She became almost a surrogate mother as my grandmother, who had suffered with rheumatic fever as a younger person, was frail with indifferent health after the birth of her fourth child. After the shock of the sudden death of Fergus my grandmother survived little more than another year.

 

Another other odd experience I had was when I began reading and transcribing these letters.   I realised that the date was January 23rd 2003, 62 years to the day since his death.  Ironically, too, Fergus had died on his sister Hazel's birthday.

 

And finally, my daughter Anne Margaret O'Leary, now a Squadron Leader (Nursing Officer) in the RAAF became a Pilot Officer at the end of 1991 and she commenced duties at the Edinburgh RAAF Base in January 1992, fifty years after Fergus' death. (His commission to the rank of Pilot Officer was given at the commencement of his active service late 1941).  Anne has since served overseas in East Timor, West Irian and briefly in the Solomon Islands.

 

At the time of writing this, and as I transcribe Fergus' letters from World War II, Australians are again and still divided about the potential involvement of our troops in yet another war, in the Middle East again, this time in Iraq.  In a letter written by Fergus early in December 1941 he makes a pertinent comment about the division then, amongst his fellow Australians, and the unwillingness of certain parties to fight in what they saw as someone else's war.  He had just heard the news of the attack on Pearl Harbour, December 7th 1941, and the bombing of Singapore and Hong Kong at the same time. Would the boys be ready now to defend their homeland?

 

It gives one an odd feeling to read these letters now, reliving the history which took place more than sixty years ago.  They reveal in some small and personal way the hopes and fears, the laughter and tears, of our brave young people in the merciless arena of war, "On Active Service."

 

Fergus' final letter, dated 16th January 1942, was written only a week before his death, and one day after the surrender of Singapore to the Japanese.    He had not known.
